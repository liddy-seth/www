No Secondary Voltage Reading (all other readings appear normal)

This condition suggests that there is a suspected failure in the secondary voltage metering system. This condition is unlikely to be related to the precipitator. It is more likely to be related to the performance of the electrical equipment. It is quite possible at this stage to assume that this is probably affecting the performance of the Precipitator. Potential side effects are:

  • Failure of the control to correctly detect sparks and arcs within the ESP.
  • Failure of the control to correctly limit its output to a preset secondary voltage level.
  • Random under voltage trips.
Note: A failure of the high voltage divider (if mounted in the TR oil) could allow arcing across a failed resistor that might degrade the insulation properties of the TR Oil.

COURSE OF ACTION:

The secondary voltage measured on the system may be displayed in two locations. You may have one or both of the following:

  • Analog Meters on the cabinet door
  • Digital readings on the Automatic Voltage control
The secondary voltage signal is typically a reference voltage signal derived across a voltage divider feedback resistor. This feedback resistor is typically located in the low voltage junction box or TR ground switch enclosure.
  • Locate the shunt resistor and measure its resistance.
  • Check the operations manual for your system and determine whether or not the resistor is set correctly. If not, set is correctly.
  • This resistor is typically calibrated to provide a voltage signal to the AVC.
  • It is also possible that the surge arrestor in the TR low voltage junction box may have shorted out and will be causing the shunt resistor to be shorted to ground.

This resistor is typically calibrated to provide a voltage signal to the AVC. The analog meter may use the same signal or may be a direct current reading device where the current that passes through the meter is proportional to the voltage on the ESP. If the analog meter does not read the calculated value correctly then the meter may be at fault. If the AVC does not read correctly, it is quite possible that the AVC is out of calibration or is malfunctioning. If the voltage is not present across the shunt resistor, replace the shunt resistor, re-calibrate, and recheck the signal. If the signal still is not present there is probably a fault within the transformer tank. There are numerous steps involved in the fault finding of the High Voltage system on an ESP TR set.   • Meggering the high voltage resistor that may be in the TR oil or the HV duct.
  • Measuring the current through the High Voltage resistor to ground.
  • Measuring the voltage across the voltage divider resistor to ground.
The last two of these tests are dangerous and should not be attempted other than by a fully trained TR technician.
